An analysis of board games: Part II - More on mechanics and themes

Posted on Sun 28 February 2016 in Analysis

This is part II in my series on analysing BoardGameGeek data. Other parts can be found here:

  • Part I: Introduction and general trends
  • Part II: More on mechanics and themes
  • Part III: Complexity bias in BGG

In Part I of this series, we explained how we generated a dataset from BoardGameGeek and explored general trends in the release rate of board games, board game ratings, complexity, mechanics and themes have changed over the years. In this post, we’ll tease out a little bit more information on the mechanics/themes associated with highly rated games as well as the co-occurance of mechanics and themes.

Mechanics

Ratings

One interesting thing we can do with the dataset we have obtained is train a multivariate regression model to predict game ratings based entirely on the mechanics present in a game. What this model generates is a set of parameters that tells us how much a game’s rating is expected to increase or decrease by, on average, due to the addition of that mechanic to the game. The results are shown in the plot below, with the black lines representing the 95% confidence intervals of the estimated impact of the inclusion of the mechanic in a game to its overall BGG rating.

Based on this analysis of the BGG data, it appears that the mechanics most strongly associated with positive game ratings are Worker Placement, Deck/Pool Building and Hex and Counter whereas the mechanics most strongly associated with negative game ratings are Roll / Spin and Move, Singing, and Rock-Paper-Scissors.

Since we looked at the mechanics that have increased in prevalence over the last couple of decades in part I of the analysis, an interesting and natural analysis to follow up on the results from the table above is to see if the mechanics that are associated with higher game ratings are also the ones that are becoming more prevalent. In other words, are game developers picking up on the mechanics that are associated with highly rated games and incorporating these preferentially?

It looks like there is weak, but statistically significant relationship between the mechanics that are becoming more popular and the mechanics that are associated with high ratings (Pearson-R = 0.44, p < 0.01). Most pertinently, the mechanic that is associated with the lowest game ratings (Roll / Spin and move) is also the mechanic that is being phased out of board games most rapidly. Mechanics that are associated with high game ratings that are becoming considerably more prevalent include Deck / Pool Building and Worker Placement. All in all, it’s probably to the benefit of the board gaming community that mechanics associated with high game ratings are becoming more prevalent since we can assume that these mechanics are inherently more engaging, interesting or in some other way more enjoyable mechanics to have in games.

Co-occurance

One of the things I was looking forward to when I got my hands on this data is to look at which mechanics co-occur most often. This gives us an idea of which combinations of mechanics occur together more often than expected (considering how prevalent the individual mechanics are) and which combinations are under-represented (occur less often than the individual mechanics would suggest). This might be of more interest to game designers rather than players, since it’s a resource that might help designers identify mechanics they could incorporate into their game that fit with other mechanics already in place, but more interestingly, it could be a challenge for designers to try to create interesting games that marry together mechanics seldom seen together. Let’s have a look at the co-occurance table.

From the co-occurance table above, the mechanics that most, and least often co-occur (relative to how much we’d expect them to co-occur by random distribution of mechanics, not in absolute terms), are shown in the tables below.

It seems like there’s a few clusters in mechanic co-occurance, but the cluster surrounding trading dominate the most co-occuring mechanics list. Besides the trading related mechanics, there are some other unsurprising combinations such as Singing with Acting, and Storytelling with role playing. Having had a look at the most co-occurring mechanics table, nothing stands out to me as being particularly surprising.

The list of mechanics not seen together often is more interesting. If any board game designers want a design challenge, it might be interesting to try to build a functional board game using a pair of these mechanics as the basis of the game. A particularly rare 3 mechanic combination is Roll / Spin and move, Hand management and Dice rolling, and a rare 4 mechanic combination is those 3 + Hex and Counter. Given that Roll / Spin and move is associated with poor game ratings, it’s probably not worth trying to include in modern games (unless you really want a design challenge), so an appropriate design challenge might be to create a board game with the Hand management, Dice rolling and Hex and Counter mechanics. If anyone actually decides to try to make a game out of these, please do let me know. I’d love to hear how that experiment played out.

Themes

Ratings

Similar to what we did the mechanics, we can also look at which themes are associated with high and low game ratings.

The top 11 themes most strongly associated with positive ratings include 10 historic war related themes, with the only non-war-related theme in the top 11 being Trains. Interestingly, the top war themes are specifically about historical wars. The modern equivalent of this theme: Modern Warfare is still associated with highly rated games, but is only about a quarter of the way down the list. People seem to really like board games based on historic wars. The bottom few are Trivia, Music and Movies / TV / Radio theme. The Fact that Music is at the bottom of this list isn’t all that surprising since we saw that Singing was one of the mechanics most strongly associated with poorly rated games.

As we did with mechanics, we can also look at the relationship between the expected ratings associated with each theme and the change in prevalence of the themes. This should tell us whether themes that are considered “good” (in the sense that they are associated with highly rated games) are becoming more prevalent over time than other themes.

Overall, there doesn’t appear to be any meaningful general relationship between the themes that are associated with highly rated games and the themes that are becoming more prevalent (Pearson-R = 0.08, p = 0.58). The only relationship of interest I can spot in this data is that the themes most strongly associated with poorly rated games (Trivia, Music, Movies / TV / Radio theme, Electronic and Racing) are all declining in prevalence reasonably quickly. Beyond that, the relationship becomes more unpredictable.

If anyone is interested in trying to create an unusual and unique game, those are theme combinations worth trying.

The Economic theme is the one that has the most strong association with particular mechanics, particularly the Trading, Stock Holding, Auction/Bidding, Set collection, Commonity speculation and Roll / Spin and Move mechanics. I suspect the last one of those is largely to do with the legacy of Monopoly and how its success influenced the design of subsequent Economic games. Other strong relationships between Themes and mechanics include the Hex and Counter mechanic’s link to war games, particularly World War II games and the link between the Trains theme and the Route/Network Building mechanic. There are several other strong theme-mechanic relationships that are not very surprising e.g. Music with Singing and Territory Building with Area Control / Area Influence, but one that stood out to me as being quite interesting the association between Fighting games and the Variable Player Powers mechanic. I suppose fighting games lend themselves well to slightly asymmetric styles of play between players.

There are also many Theme-Mechanic combinations that are under-explored, but some interesting ones include Trivia with Hand Management, Economic with Hex-and-Counter (Scythe does this to some extent, but it would be interesting to see more games in which moving resources and workers around a hex grid is a more integral part of the game experience) and Deduction with Dice rolling.
